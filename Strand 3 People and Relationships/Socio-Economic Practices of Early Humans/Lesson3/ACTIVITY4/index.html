Activity Introduction

Time for a challenge, Stone Age explorer! Let us see how well you remember the tools you have been studying. Piece together this jigsaw puzzle of Stone Age tools and spot the ones you can name. Each piece is part of the story. Ready? Let us put the past back together one piece at a time!

Learner Instructions
  1. Look at the puzzle board, it is waiting for you to rebuild the picture of Stone Age tools.
  2. Drag and drop each puzzle piece into the correct spot.
  3. Keep going until all the pieces are in place and the full picture is revealed.
  4. Try to name each tool as you complete it to test your memory.
Here is a Hint
  • Edge pieces are highlighted in a soft outline.
  • Tool silhouettes subtly appear in the background as hints.

Tools Summary
Tool Use & Description
Hand AxeOne of the earliest all-purpose tools! Used for chopping wood, butchering meat, and general cutting.
ScraperHelped early humans prepare animal hides and shape tools. Perfect for scraping meat off bones or cleaning animal skins.
MicrolithTiny but mighty often part of arrowheads or spears. Miniature blades fixed into wood or bone handles.
HammerstoneUsed to break nuts or shape other stones. A simple round stone used like a hammer.
Grinding StoneHelped turn nuts and grains into flour. Used to crush grains, seeds, and nuts into a meal.
Digging StickVital for planting and foraging. A simple pointed stick used to dig up tubers or holes.
Arrowhead (Hafted)Helped early humans hunt from a distance. A sharp, pointed stone often hafted onto a wooden shaft.
Polished AxeA huge improvement in Late Stone Age toolmaking! Ground smooth and used for chopping trees.

Key Takeaways

  • Stone Age tools had different shapes, sizes, and materials based on their purpose – cutting, scraping, pounding, or digging.
  • Microliths were advanced tools that were combined with handles to create composite tools like arrows or spears.
  • Illustrating and labelling tools helps learners visualize their use and importance in early human life.
  • Tool-making was a creative and critical skill that allowed early humans to adapt, survive, and eventually build communities.
  • Visual observation and drawing can improve memory, understanding, and appreciation for historical technologies.
